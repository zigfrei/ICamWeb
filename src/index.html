Manual Turning Movement Counts

Traffic counts (Vehicles (Cars, Light and Medium Trucks, Heavy Trucks, Buses, Pedestrians, etc.) passing through a point, or through an intersection. The data can be recorded in a variety of ways with the use of TFR-2 Gretch boards. Traffic counts can be used in traffic impact studies or evaluation of traffic signal warrants analyses among others.

While administering manual turning movement counts, proper procedures are essential for attaining accurate results. We have a rigorous quality control program in place and all our surveyors receive proper and thorough training to make sure that all client requirements are satisfied.

Data collection options include up to six custom classes per any time period bin to be surveyed. Usually, data is accumulated by fifteen minute intervals. Results are generally presented electronically, in an easy to read format, depending on client specifications.

Automatic Traffic Recorder (ATR) Counts

ATR counting is the solution for gathering large amounts of traffic volume data throughout specific timeframe which can range from a more general 24-hour counts to an extended 168+ hours periods. ATR counts are undertaken by means of pneumatic tubes that measure the direction of flow, traffic speed, vehicle classification and other crucial variables.

Automatic Traffic Recorders (ATRs) offer a quick, affordable, and reliable way to collect vehicle data over any period and can be presented in a range of formats to suit individual client requirements.

Automatic traffic count data is used for a variety of purposes to support transport planning and design considerations. It is a highly cost effective method of collecting large volumes of classified and/ or speed data. Accu-Traffic has always been at the forefront of the market for collecting and presenting this data.

Travel Time Studies

Travel Time studies are used to document congestion and to quantify of the actual impact of road improvements. They help to monitor the performance of the roadway system, and observe trends in vehicle travel time, delay and congestion during peak travel periods. Most travel time study techniques involve using GPS equipment receivers which are able to automatically record distance, time, and speed at any given time on the road.

The purposes of travel time studies are to identify problem locations in area, determine the level of service of the facility, and provide travel time for economic evaluation of transportation improvement and information for travellers.

Most common techniques for travel time studies are floating-car approach, licence-plate approach and GPS based approach.

Gap, Queue, Left Turn and Delay Studies

Delay is defined as an extra time spent by drivers against their expectation. Delay can have many forms depending on different locations. Accurate estimation of delays has many implications in traffic engineering studies. Transportation planners will use delay to evaluate economic loses, whereas traffic engineers will use delays to determine level of service at signalized intersections. There are several methods of conducting intersection delays: Licence-plate method, sample-counting or cycle-based method.

Spot Speed Studies

Speed studies are used primarily to determine the distribution of traffic speeds, or vehicle speed/class percentiles, at specific location.

Spot speed – defined as the average speed of vehicles passing a point. It is the mostly used indicator that traffic engineers use to measure the speed that drivers freely select, meaning that spot speed studies are under free flow conditions. Spot speed collection methods include: Radar meters, stop-watch method, loop detector, video detector.

Pedestrian Counts

There are many reasons for pedestrian counts. Counts are often required in the street design process as inputs to transport models, for capacity assessments, to measure the attractiveness of different spaces, to estimate route choices or for the appraisal of different design options.

Pedestrians are more difficult to count than motor vehicles or bicycles because their paths are much less constrained. Pedestrians can stand, pace back and forth, use a wheelchair, or walk by in groups with no light gaps in-between them to be detected by a counter.

While automated counting technology for motor vehicles has been in use for many years, automated pedestrian counting technology is less developed. Most pedestrian counts are done manually (see photo), but this method suffers from variations in quality depending on the reliability of the person counting. Automated counting would be preferred if a reliable technology, or group of technologies can be deployed.
